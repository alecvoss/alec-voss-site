Electric Shock
Drowning

The invisible cause of death filed as a drowning. What every swimmer needs to know before entering fresh water near a dock.

How it happens
1

A faulty dock leaks AC current into the water

Wiring faults or improperly grounded generators send alternating current into the water around docks and marinas. No sound. No visible sign. No smell.

2

Fresh water holds the current invisibly

Unlike salt water, fresh water conducts at a level enough to paralyze muscles. The field radiates outward — strongest near the dock, weakening with distance.

3

A swimmer enters the field and can no longer move

As little as 3 volts per foot causes skeletal muscle paralysis. Arms stop. Legs stop. Cannot call for help. They sink. Cause of death on the paperwork: drowning.

Warning signs
Act on this immediately
Any tingling, burning, or sudden muscle weakness near a dock is a possible ESD field. Most people swim toward the ladder. This is exactly wrong. Moving toward the source moves you deeper into the field.
Signal 01

Tingling in hands, feet, or legs

Not cold numbness. A distinct electrical sensation. Get out immediately.

Signal 02

Sudden muscle weakness

Arms or legs feel heavy. Not fatigue. An abrupt loss of limb control.

Signal 03

Swimmer in distress near dock

Rigid body in water. Do not jump in. Shut off power first.

Signal 04

Fish kills near a dock

Fish floating with no explanation. ESD fields affect fish before humans.

Swimmer safety protocol

Feel tingling — swim parallel, not toward

Exit the field by swimming parallel to the dock and away. Do not grab the ladder. Call for someone on shore to cut the power first.

Someone in distress — cut the power first

Do not enter the water. Find the power disconnect box and shut it off. Then go in. The rescuer at Lake Lanier felt the current — he survived because he cut the power first.

Before swimming — find the power box

Locate the main power disconnect before anyone gets in. Point it out to one person who stays on shore. The single most important step.

Never swim alone near docks

An ESD victim cannot call for help. One person on shore is the only rescue mechanism available.

Swim only in designated zones

Distance from powered infrastructure is your primary protection. At Lake Lanier, use only Army Corps designated swim areas.

For dock owners
Action 01

Install a ShockIQ monitoring device

$300–$500 installed. Alarms if voltage is detected in dock water.

Action 02

Install ground-fault circuit interrupters

ABYC-certified electrician. GFCIs on all dock power pedestals.

Action 03

Annual electrical inspection

Wiring degrades. No mandatory inspection exists in most US states.

Action 04

Post a disconnect sign

Power disconnect must be clearly labelled and visible. Not locked.
